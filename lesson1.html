Lesson 1: Introduction to Propositional Logic

Beyond Aristotelian Logic: A Brief Overview

While this course begins with classical propositional logic—which finds its roots in Aristotelian philosophy and even ancient wisdom like the biblical injunction to "Let your 'Yes' be 'Yes,' and your 'No,' 'No'" (Matthew 5:37)—it's important to recognize that logic extends far beyond these binary foundations.

"Let your 'Yes' be 'Yes,' and your 'No,' 'No.' For whatever is more than these is from the evil one."
— Matthew 5:37 (NKJV)

This ancient wisdom emphasizes clarity and truthfulness in communication, reflecting the binary nature of classical logic where statements are either true or false. However, modern logic has developed many systems that handle more complex and nuanced reasoning:

Many-Valued Logic

Extends beyond true/false to include additional truth values like "unknown" or "possible." Fuzzy logic, used in control systems, allows for degrees of truth between 0 and 1.

Intuitionistic Logic

Rejects the law of excluded middle (either P or not P), requiring constructive proof of statements. Important in computer science and mathematics.

Paraconsistent Logic

Allows contradictions without making every statement provable. Useful for managing inconsistent information in databases and AI systems.

Despite these advanced systems, classical propositional logic remains the foundation for most mathematical reasoning and computer science applications. Its simplicity and clarity make it the ideal starting point for studying formal logic.

What is Logic?

Logic is the systematic study of reasoning and argument. It provides the tools to distinguish correct reasoning from incorrect reasoning. We can define it as:

Logic is the process of deducing information correctly.

The validity of an argument depends on its syntax (its structure or form), not necessarily on the semantics (the actual truth or meaning) of the statements involved.

The Building Blocks: Propositions

A proposition is a declarative statement that is unequivocally either true (T) or false (F).

Examples of Propositions:

  • T: \(\sqrt{2}\) is a real number.
  • T: \(2 + 3 = 5\).
  • F: All integers are even.
  • F: \(\sqrt{3}\) is an integer.

Non-Examples (What is not a proposition?):

  • Open Sentences: \(x + 7 = 13\) (Its truth value depends on \(x\)).
  • Questions: Are polynomials continuous?
  • Commands: Close the door.
  • Exclamations: Wow!

Arguments and Validity

An argument consists of premises and a conclusion. An argument is valid if the conclusion necessarily follows from the premises. Crucially, a valid argument can have false premises and a false conclusion—it's about the logical connection, not the actual truth.

Example of a Valid Argument:

  • Premise 1: If it is raining, then I will bring an umbrella.
  • Premise 2: It is raining.
  • Conclusion: Therefore, I will bring an umbrella.

This form is valid. If the premises are true, the conclusion must be true.

Example of an Invalid Argument:

  • Premise 1: If it is raining, then I will bring an umbrella.
  • Premise 2: I will bring an umbrella.
  • Conclusion: Therefore, it is raining.

This is invalid. You could be bringing an umbrella for another reason (e.g., bright sun). The form is flawed.

And here’s the twist: an argument can be valid even if it’s completely false in content. For instance: ‘Socrates is a Martian. All Martians live on Pluto. Therefore, Socrates lives on Pluto.’ Every single statement is false — but the argument form is valid.

Point to ponder

An argument is valid if and only if it is impossible for all the premises to be true while the conclusion is false

De Morgan's Laws

These crucial laws describe how negation distributes over conjunction and disjunction.

  1. Negation of AND: \(\neg(P \land Q)\) is equivalent to \(\neg P \lor \neg Q\).
    "It is not the case that both P and Q are true" means "At least one of P or Q is false."
  2. Negation of OR: \(\neg(P \lor Q)\) is equivalent to \(\neg P \land \neg Q\).
    "It is not the case that either P or Q is true" means "Both P and Q are false."

Truth Table Proof of De Morgan's First Law:

PQ\(P \land Q\)\(\neg(P \land Q)\) \(\neg P\)\(\neg Q\)\(\neg P \lor \neg Q\)
TTTFFFF
TFFTFTT
FTFTTFT
FFFTTTT

Since the columns for \(\neg(P \land Q)\) and \(\neg P \lor \neg Q\) are identical, the statements are logically equivalent.

Conditional Statements (Implications)

The statement "If P, then Q" is called a conditional or implication and is written \(P \rightarrow Q\).

Example 1:

"If you clean your room (P), then you will get ice cream (Q)."

The only way this promise is broken is if the room is cleaned (P is T) but no ice cream is given (Q is F).

Biconditional Statements

The statement "P if and only if Q" is called a biconditional and is written \(P \leftrightarrow Q\). It means that P and Q have the exact same truth value.

Summary of Connectives

Connective Symbol Meaning
Negation ¬ not
Conjunction ∧ and
Disjunction ∨ or (inclusive)
Conditional → if... then...
Biconditional ↔ if and only if
