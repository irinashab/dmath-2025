Definition of Relations

Definition: Let $X$ and $Y$ be sets. A binary relation $R$ from $X$ to $Y$ is a subset of $X \times Y$, that is, a set of ordered pairs $(x, y)$ where $x \in X$ and $y \in Y$.

  • $X$ is the domain of $R$, $Y$ is the codomain
  • If $X = Y$, we say $R$ is a "relation on $X$"
  • We can think of it as a formula $R(x, y)$
  • We write $x \, R \, y$ for $(x, y) \in R$ or $R(x, y)$

Examples of Relations

Order Relations

Classical example: $x, y \in \mathbb{Z}$, $R$ is $<$:

  • $0 < 1$ means "0 is less than 1"
  • Adds structure to $\mathbb{Z}$
  • Sets have no structure: just elements
  • Relations add structure: how elements relate to each other

Rock, Paper, Scissors

Let $X = \{\text{rock, paper, scissors}\}$. Let $x \, R \, y$ mean "$x$ beats $y$ in a game of rock/paper/scissors".

$R$ completely described:

  • rock beats scissors
  • scissors beats paper
  • paper beats rock

So $R = \{(\text{rock, scissors}), (\text{scissors, paper}), (\text{paper, rock})\}$

Properties of Relations

Let $R$ be a relation on set $X$:

  • Reflexive: $\forall x(x \, R \, x)$
  • Symmetric: $\forall x\forall y(x \, R \, y \rightarrow y \, R \, x)$
  • Transitive: $\forall x\forall y\forall z(x \, R \, y \land y \, R \, z \rightarrow x \, R \, z)$
  • Anti-symmetric: $\forall x\forall y(x \, R \, y \land y \, R \, x \rightarrow x = y)$
  • Asymmetric: $\forall x\forall y(x \, R \, y \rightarrow \neg(y \, R \, x))$

Equivalence Relations

Definition: A relation $\sim$ on $X$ is an equivalence relation if it is:

  1. Reflexive: $\forall x \in X: x \sim x$
  2. Symmetric: $\forall x,y \in X: x \sim y \rightarrow y \sim x$
  3. Transitive: $\forall x,y,z \in X: x \sim y \land y \sim z \rightarrow x \sim z$

Equivalence relations generalize notions of "sameness":

  • $x = y$
  • $x \equiv y \pmod{n}$
  • Cardinality: $|A| = |B|$
  • Congruent shapes, similar triangles, etc.

Example: Congruence modulo 3 is an equivalence relation on $\mathbb{Z}$.

Equivalence Classes

Definition: Let $\sim$ be an equivalence relation on $X$.

  • The equivalence class of $x$ is $[x]_{\sim} = \{y \in X : x \sim y\}$
  • The quotient of $X$ by $\sim$ is $X/\sim = \{[x]_{\sim} : x \in X\}$
  • $x$ is called a representative of its equivalence class $[x]_{\sim}$

Modular Arithmetic Examples

  • $X = \mathbb{Z}$, $\sim$ is $\equiv \pmod{2}$:
    • Equivalence classes: evens and odds
    • $X/\sim = \{[0]_{\sim}, [1]_{\sim}\}$
  • $X = \mathbb{Z}$, $\sim$ is $\equiv \pmod{10}$:
    • Equivalence classes: numbers with same last digit
    • $X/\sim = \{[0]_{\sim}, [1]_{\sim}, \ldots, [9]_{\sim}\}$

Squares Example

Let $X = \mathbb{Z}$, and $x \sim y$ if $x^2 = y^2$.

Claim: $\sim$ is an equivalence relation.

Proof: Follows from reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity of $=$.

  • $[0]_{\sim} = \{0\}$
  • $[1]_{\sim} = \{1, -1\}$
  • $[2]_{\sim} = \{2, -2\}$
  • There is one equivalence class for each non-negative integer
  • Good set of representatives: $\mathbb{N} \cup \{0\}$

Sine Example

Let $X = \mathbb{R}$ and $x \sim y$ if $\sin(x) = \sin(y)$.

Claim: $\sim$ is an equivalence relation.

Proof: Follows from reflexivity/symmetry/transitivity of $=$.

  • $[0]_{\sim} = \{k\pi : k \in \mathbb{Z}\}$
  • $[\pi/2]_{\sim} = \{\pi/2 + 2\pi k : k \in \mathbb{Z}\}$
  • There is one equivalence class for each value in the range $[-1, 1]$ of sine
  • Good set of representatives: $[-\pi/2, \pi/2]$ (each $y$-value appears exactly once)
